Investing Advice

A few quick beliefs that guide my investing acumen –

  • Only invest in stocks or bonds. Home ownership [1], gold, Bitcoin, etc. are all scams long-term.
  • Insider trading is rampant. Never buy individual stocks. Only index funds like VOO.
  • The best companies remain private. Public companies focus on nothing but the next quarter to appease shareholders. So any individual stock you can buy is likely a long-term loser.
  • Remember the Cantillon Effect – those closest to the money supply eat first and eat best.
  • Check if interest rates are at long-term highs or lows. If low, dollar-cost average into VOO [2]. When high (like present), cash out and buy treasury bills for a 4-5% annual return.
  • As a rule, never enter a position unless you're comfortable holding it for at least a year. (Trading is foolish and short-term capital gains tax will gut your earnings even if you get lucky.)
  • Warren Buffett has sold his Apple stock while tech jobs have collapsed. This isn't good.
  • The Israel-Iran conflict may also be spiraling out of control. In the likely event it escalates, oil prices and interest rates will skyrocket while the markets crash.
  • Keep in mind that stock market returns from 1971 to 2018 were predicated on globalization and good relations with China. With Trump, this may no longer be possible.

[1] Most people can only afford to buy one house – the one they live in. If you purchase a house for $500,000 and the price increases to a million, you've made money on paper, but all the other houses in your neighborhood have increased in price by the same amount, so you'll have to purchase a new house at the same inflated price (you need to live somewhere). Meaning you're breaking even at best. After interest payments and maintenance costs and realtor fees and property taxes, you've lost money.

[2] Be very careful when transitioning back into stocks from bonds. The worst of the 2008 crash happened after the pivot to lower rates. The Federal Reserve started cutting interest rates in 2007, and by January 30, 2008, it had reduced short-term interest rates to 3%, down from 5.25% in September 2007. Lehman Brothers collapsed in September 2008.

Political Views
